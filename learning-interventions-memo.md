# Build retrieval-first sessions with step-level coaching and visible progress

**Give step-level, high-information feedback on every answer.** This is the largest design lever in the memo. Step-level tutoring (responding to each reasoning step, not just the final answer) achieves d = 0.76, near human tutors (d = 0.79); answer-level systems achieve d = 0.31 (VanLehn 2011, meta-analysis). The step-vs-answer gap (0.45 SD) dwarfs the human-vs-computer gap (0.03 SD). Kestin et al. (2025) Harvard RCT confirmed with an AI tutor: d = 0.63-1.3 vs. active learning classroom — that tutor had pre-written solutions in its prompt and revealed them incrementally. On top of step-level granularity, make feedback high-information: address what the student did (task), how they approached it (process), and whether they're monitoring their own thinking (self-regulation). Wisniewski et al. (2020) meta-analyzed 435 studies: high-information feedback produces d = 0.99 vs. d = 0.46 for correctness-only and d = 0.24 for reinforcement alone. Practice tests with feedback produce d = 0.67-0.72; without feedback, d = 0.35-0.47 (Rowland 2014; Adesope 2017).
"The key difference is the granularity of interaction — whether tutoring occurs at the step level or the answer level."
— [VanLehn (2011), Educational Psychologist](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/00461520.2011.611369) · A1
"Feedback is more effective the more information it contains."
— [Wisniewski, Zierer & Hattie (2020), Frontiers in Psychology](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6987456/) · A1

**Default to 60-80% retrieval practice.** Retrieval practice has g = 0.50 over restudying across three independent meta-analyses (Rowland 2014: 159 comparisons; Adesope 2017: 272 effects; Yang 2021: classroom settings). Roediger & Karpicke (2006, Exp. 2): study-once-test-thrice retained 61% at one week vs. 40% for study-four-times. Karpicke & Roediger (2008): repeated testing produced ~80% recall at one week vs. ~36% when items left the testing pool after first correct recall. Shift toward more intake when accuracy drops below ~50%. Dunlosky et al. (2013) rated practice testing and distributed practice as the only two "high utility" techniques out of 10 reviewed.
"Repeated studying after learning had no effect on delayed recall, but repeated testing produced a large positive effect."
— [Karpicke & Roediger (2008), Science](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18276894/) · A1

**Match practice type to knowledge type.** For procedural skills (chain rule, standard problems), start novices with worked examples (d = 0.70-1.00 vs. problem-solving; Sweller & Cooper 1985), then fade by removing steps (d = 0.40-0.50 over pure worked examples; Renkl et al. 2002), then independent problems with step-level feedback. This reverses for experts: worked examples hurt by d = -0.30 to -0.50 (Kalyuga et al. 2001). For conceptual understanding (why chain rule works, what eigenvectors mean), use productive failure: have the student attempt before instruction, then teach. Sinha & Kapur (2021) meta-analyzed 53 studies: d = 0.36 overall, d = 0.58 for high-fidelity implementations. Does nothing for procedural fluency (d = 0.05, n.s.). Follow with self-explanation prompts ("why does this make sense?"): d = 0.55 across 69 studies (Bisra et al. 2018). Pure discovery without consolidation is consistently worse (Kirschner, Sweller & Clark 2006). For factual recall (definitions, dates, formulas), use free-response retrieval practice; short-answer beats MCQ by d = 0.19-0.50 (Kang, McDaniel & Pashler 2007; Butler & Roediger 2007).
"The worked example effect is among the most replicated findings in educational psychology, with typical effect sizes of d = 0.70-0.80 for novices."
— [Sweller (2006), Learning and Instruction](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.learninstruc.2006.02.005) · A1
"Overall effect of productive failure on conceptual knowledge: d = 0.36 [95% CI: 0.20, 0.51]."
— [Sinha & Kapur (2021), Review of Educational Research](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.3102/00346543211019105) · A2

**Interleave related topics within a session.** The mechanism is discrimination: Kang & Pashler (2012) found spacing alone gave zero benefit, while interleaving alone worked fully. Rohrer et al. (2020) RCT (787 students, 54 classes, 5 schools): interleaved 61% vs. blocked 38% one month later (d = 0.83). Interleaving fails when categories are unrelated, before basics are mastered, or for pure vocabulary. Practical model from Rohrer et al. (2015): each assignment has a small block on the new skill plus mixed review of earlier skills.
"Temporal spacing alone produced no benefit. Simultaneous display of different categories was as effective as interleaving."
— [Kang & Pashler (2012), Applied Cognitive Psychology](https://tdlc.ucsd.edu/SV2012/Pubs/Kang&Pashler(2012,ACP).pdf) · A2

**Use Anki for distributed practice across days.** Distributed practice is one of two "high utility" techniques in Dunlosky et al. (2013) and one of the most replicated findings in psychology: Cepeda et al. (2006) meta-analyzed 317 experiments and found d = 0.42 over massed practice. Optimal gap is ~10-20% of the retention interval (Cepeda et al. 2008). Anki handles this automatically via spaced repetition scheduling. Auto-generate cards from successful retrieval attempts during sessions; batch-review in a separate 10-minute pass. Each card tests one fact (minimum information principle). Only card what you understand. The generation effect (d = 0.40) applies because the student produced the knowledge during retrieval.
Rule 1: "Do not learn if you do not understand."
— [Wozniak (1999), SuperMemo 20 Rules](https://www.supermemo.com/en/blog/twenty-rules-of-formulating-knowledge) · B2

**Start every new test with a diagnostic pretest; move topics to review after 3 correct retrievals.** The pretest calibrates difficulty and tells the system whether to assign worked examples or independent practice per topic. Even failed retrieval attempts prime the brain for subsequent learning (pretesting effect; Richland et al. 2009). For transition: Rawson & Dunlosky (2011) found three correct recalls was optimal; four gave no additional benefit. After 3 correct retrievals, a topic moves from active drilling to interleaved review.

**Design for immediate reinforcement: per-question feedback, visible progress on every screen, one-tap session start, zero decisions.** ADHD reduces dopamine signaling in reward pathways (Volkow et al. 2009, JAMA), steepening the delay-of-reinforcement gradient (Sonuga-Barke 2002). Gamification with immediate feedback produces g = 0.49 on cognitive outcomes (Sailer & Homner 2020). Punitive mechanics (hearts, lives) trigger emotional dysregulation and accelerate abandonment (Shaw et al. 2014). Defaults with opt-out preserve autonomy while reducing decision load (Patall et al. 2008: choice enhances motivation at d = 0.40, but too many choices hurt).
"Adults with ADHD had lower dopamine synaptic markers in the left side of the caudate and in the limbic regions than controls."
— [Volkow et al. (2009), JAMA](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19738093/) · A1

**Do not build around Socratic questioning.** No rigorous effect size exists. The only head-to-head RCT (Rose et al. 2001) had N=20 and p>.10. Collins et al.'s "model, coach, scaffold, fade" was theoretical (Admiralty: C4). The Kestin AI tutor (d = 0.63-1.3) used embedded solutions revealed step by step, not Socratic questioning.

## Risks & Mitigations

- Interleaving feels worse than blocking (students rate it less effective despite 2x retention). Explain upfront; let progress data prove it. Interleave only after one successful retrieval pass per topic, and only across related topics.
- LLMs over-intervene. In MetaCLASS benchmarking ([arxiv 2602.02457](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.02457v1)), the best model predicted "no intervention" 4.2% of the time when silence was appropriate 41.7% of the time. The prompt must enforce restraint.
- 80% retrieval with near-zero domain knowledge means 80% failure. Detect low accuracy early and shift to worked examples until accuracy reaches ~50%.
- Voice-rambling creates infinite loops. After two vague attempts on a step, model the answer and move on.
- Single-day prep has a hard ceiling. Cramming produces 50-80% forgetting within days. State this upfront.
